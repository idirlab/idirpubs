Title Crowdsourcing Pareto-Optimal Object Finding by Pairwise Comparisons
Masked Reviewer ID: Assigned_Reviewer_1
Review:
Question 
Overall Rating Undecided
Short explanation for the rating (1 paragraph) The paper considers the selection of objects which are “pareto-optimal” (not dominated by any other object) after crowdsourcing a number of rank-based questions about their properties (scaled attributes, higher numbers correspond to higher quality). The method targets the minimization of pairwise comparisons between objects, through an iterative process of question answering; during the crowdsourcing query process, possible contradictions in the ranking are pragmatically solved, by either ignoring some questions or by conveniently changing query outcomes. Several heuristics for query micro-ordering are presented and evaluated thoroughly.
The paper is well-written and clear. It has a main problem, however. Validation has been only done based on simulation, without crowsourcing queries. Being myself a researcher in the crowdsourching field, I don’t believe that simulation can effectively report crowd-based interactions, which are more complex than what can be achieved through simulation.
Detailed Comments (describe the main result, positive and negative aspects, comment on contribution) The paper presents a number of variant methods and then compares them from the viewpoint of "performance", which is the number of questions for getting an ordering. But what about the "quality" of the result? Does it make sense to build a notion of quality? If not, the authors should explain why. --- Of course, given that the method simulates the human behavior, this is a question that is hard to answer based on the material which is provided in the paper, but I do assume that authors started from some real problem and tested its relevance with real data. Right???
If not accepted, what should the authors do to improve the paper? Essentially, provide real crowd-based validation

Question 
Overall Rating Undecided
Short explanation for the rating (1 paragraph) The paper is well presented and the explanations, though hard to follow due to the formal definitions, seem solid and interesting. However, the work seems out of focus with respect to EDBT scope, authors should have put more effort to show why their work is interesting for EDBT community, instead of just saying that it is related to crowdsourcing (only?)
Detailed Comments (describe the main result, positive and negative aspects, comment on contribution) The paper presents a solution to find Pareto-optimal objects, which is described many times as of great interest for crowdsourcing systems. The paper is hard to follow, even introduction, due to the formalism used, but this is not bad at all; perhaps more real examples could help.
Instead, the work seems very solid, including related work section, details of their approach, and experiments performed. However, my main concerns are about whether this work is interesting for the EDBT community or not. No effort is spent in presenting its interest for people not working on crowdsourcing. I dont believe this solution about finding Pareto- optimal objects is only valid for crowdsourcing. Hence, I would like to know what crowdsourcing means for the authors: definitely there exist MANY kinds of crowdsourcing that do not consist in requesting people to choose between choices but to perfom certain tasks in a distributed manner, for example (something out of the scope of this work). In other words, the application context of this work has not been defined as precisely as the solution they propose
If not accepted, what should the authors do to improve the paper? Perhaps it is a perfect paper for a conference focused on computing/algorithms, I see their proposal very general, which is very good. In general I think the authors should show that their proposal goes beyond crowdsourcing, they never say a word about its application to other areas, but their approach seems too general for just one application context (i.e. crowdsourcing). If focused only on crowdsourcing for any reason, the paper should include a good description of what they mean by crowdsourcing, this is a must. Considering it was submitted to EDBT, they definitely should have shown the interest of their work for people dealing with database technology or management of big amounts of data
Masked Reviewer ID: Assigned_Reviewer_3
Review:
Question 
Overall Rating Undecided
Short explanation for the rating (1 paragraph) This paper use crowdsourcing to find Pareto-optimal objects by building up
the relationship between objects on different criteria as a partial order.
The strength is in reducing the number of questions asked and thus reducing
cost, making use of the dominating aspect of partially classified objects.
The weakness is on the simple treatment of conflicts, and the difficulty
to make the crowdsourcing really distributed, as there is the assumption of
completion of the rlt for a question before selecting the next one and this
requires more or less lockstep answering by workers.
Detailed Comments (describe the main result, positive and negative aspects, comment on contribution) Does Pareto-Optimal Object form the skyline? Is it just because you use
partial order while skyline relies on total ordering attributes? But
skyline can also work in partial order [10, 26 etc], so what is the
difference? This should be made clear. Basically I can see that a major
distinction is the behavior of the crowd is not deterministic, unlike
skyline-based precise values and inconsistency could arise due to voting.

It is not clear whether it is too realistic to assume sequential question
asking. When is rlt(qi) known? Is it after sufficient people have answered
qi, or just that particular helper answered qi? It relies on the way rlt is
defined. The former is not realistic, and the later is fine. Fig 3 seems
to suggest the former, but why you cannot continue asking questions for
fast responders, especially in the initial stage to speed up the process,
with a small risk of overspending?

Overriding conflicts leads to efficiency, but it may also hide some important
information. If x > y and y > z but you then find z > x, it might well be
x > y (or y > z) that is wrong (it could be y > x). Without additional info,
the "probability" that z > x is wrong is lower than the combined probability
that one of the other two is wrong. Would it be useful to look at the "strength"
of those > relation (value beyond theta)? A discussion on this issue (and may
be some quantification in the experiments) would be very useful.

In section 5.1, the way the perturbation is generated does not create any
possibility of conflict. The actual unit used in the exponent also affects
the result. A difference of 0.5 point in scoring is not much, but 0.5 block
would be far more impactful. How are you going to normalize the value of x.c
and y.c? A modification to introduce conflict also allows its study in some
way. The lack of conflict also does not correspond too well to real application
in ranking pictures etc.

In section 5.2.2, out of 15 workers, 5 of them fail the validation check
(for just 2 questions, and one is pretty straightforward as stated) and
this is on the high side. Is the second question "hard"? If not, then the
workers themselves may demand a closer look.

It is not necessary to define partial order, which is a generic concept
that could be cited with a maths text.

Only one counter-example is sufficient to prove property 1, it is not
necessary to give two (to save space).

The names of macro- and micro-ordering look like an overkill, and may be
confusing, especially when compared to macro- and micro-average in IR and
PR areas. Yours is just two-level ordering (like primary versus secondary
sorting key in Excel).

Some typos, e.g. "less comparisons" -> "fewer comparisons".
If not accepted, what should the authors do to improve the paper? Clarify relationship with skyline with partial order, and put in more
effort in inconsistency resolution and plan for deployment to actual crowds.
Please see (3) above.
