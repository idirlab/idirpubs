List of Strong Points (1) An interesting application of skyline techniques in the context of ‘computational journalism’.
(2) Well-motivated pruning techniques.
(3) Good summary of existing work on skyline variants.
List of Weak Points (1) A very similar idea (with a rather different solution) has been proposed earlier (see pointer below), though the authors may not have been aware of this work. However, they do need to cite the earlier paper.
(2) There is no real evaluation of the proposed ranking scheme, except for a handful of examples and it is not clear how well it really works. If nothing else, the authors should have discussed other ranking approaches in this context.
Detailed Comments The authors propose an approach to discover prominent situational facts using skyline operators. The challenge here is that they need to perform this operation all possible measure subspaces, be able to include constraints and rank the different resulting tuples. They propose approaches to deal with each of these challenges, mainly based on subspace-pruning; the ranking part is not as convincing.

While many of the ideas described in this paper are (variants of) known approaches, the algorithmic choices strike me as sensible and well-argued.

Additional comments:
- I would like then authors to comment on similarities and contrast their work to this paper: Foteini Alvanaki, Evica Ilieva, Sebastian Michel, Aleksandar Stupar. Interesting Event Detection through Hall of Fame Rankings. Third ACM SIGMOD Workshop on Databases and Social Networks (DBSOCIAL 2013), in conjunction with SIGMOD 2013, New York, NY, USA.

Detailed Comments This paper introduces a new problem, that of finding the measures and constraints of tuples that are in contextual skyline. I believe this work will generate interest in the community, and can open the door for ideas on how to push this further and how to apply it to slightly different applications and scenarios.

The motivation section should be stronger, with examples from at least a couple of different applications that can make use of this.

List of Strong Points
- formalizes a problem of incremental contextual skyline construction
- develops several search algorithms with well designed pruning strategies that significantly outperform baselines and existing work adapted to this new problem
List of Weak Points
- skylines are an active area of research, and this work does not seem like a significant leap forward
- given the potential blowup of possible skylines, the time and space efficiency of the algorithm do not scale well with the size of the table (esp. dimensionality)
- the problem being addressed is interesting, but less clear whether this formalization captures its essence and leads to good solutions.
Detailed Comments
This paper proposes new problem, which is a variant of a skyline problem: as each tuple arrives, determine whether it is part of any *contextual* skyline for some measure subspace. The context is determined by filtering the table based on any subset of attributes. The paper proposes both top down and bottom up search approaches that include various pruning strategies that are proven to only prune non-matches.

- There is a potential for a huge blowup of contextual skylines, especially as the dimensionality of the domain increases (more attributes or higher cardinality attribute domains). The paper introduces a prominence measure, however, this is a post-processing step that filters the results of the algorithm. The algorithm must still generate all contextual skylines and may run out of memory doing so (esp. bottom up approaches, which require materialization). Furthermore, I'm not entirely convinced the output that the prominence threshold is enough to manage the flood of potential skylines that the end user must examine.

- The problem is motivated by real-world problems, but it's not entirely convincing that the formalization captures the essence of those problems. To take the sports world, for instance, often the newsworthy statistics require aggregation over a set of recent tuples (e.g., Federer's loss in the US Open makes this the first season since 2002 that he did not reach at least one grand slam final).
