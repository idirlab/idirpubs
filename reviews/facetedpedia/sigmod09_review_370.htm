Question 
Summary of the paper's main contributions and impact The paper proposes algorithms for automatically producing hierarchies of categories characterizing a given set of Wikipedia articles - more specifically, the result of a given query, but the algorithms consider "A set of articles", regardless of how they are obtained.
The special characteristic of this work is to identify these hierarchies dynamically (at query times).
The work relies is based on an assumption which may be wrong; however, the authors do not consider the risk that falsifying this assumption brings to their approach.
Three strong points of the paper (please number them S1,S2,S3) S1. The paper tackles an interesting problem - how to automatically derive categories that help the user set in context a given set of articles.
Three weak points of the paper (please number them W1,W2,W3) W1. The paper's algorithms rely on a crucial hypothesis: that the links comprised in an article refer to categories (attributes) characterizing the article. This may or may not be true. For instance,the links of an article may refer to similar entities. The whole construction of the paper relies on this assumption, yet no consideration is given to the degree to which results may be corrupted, should this assumption turn out to be false.
W2. The paper seems immature; it has a feeling of "we tried this, we tried that". Trying is excellent, but the reader would deserve more thoughts of what happened, how and why. This may come by maturing the work a bit more.
W3. The paper's writing degrades from the beginning towards the end, in style and clarity.

Detailed comments (please number each point) D1. Any chance this approach may serve other sites than Wikipedia? Perhaps Factbase, Cyc?... connections to any Semantic Web data set?

D2. In the experimental evaluation, it is not clear whether each point (evaluation, comparison) that you do is obtain by exactly one Mechanical Turk Human Intelligence Task. In other words, does that correspond to one single click? If yes, then the evaluation is quite brittle - perhaps a different user would have responded differently.

D3. In the experimental section, text seems to have been stuffed using negative spaces.

D4. The writing could be improved and clarified in many places. Here are some comments that came to my mind as I read.

Section 1, "Challenge 1": you say "the user could then navigate to Paul Erdos" - could then navigate, from where? we are already in Paul Erdos' page.
"by form of an government" -> "by form of government"

Section 2, under "(A) By Types...": the paragraph "Our scenario and approach are unique... discovery" is unclear. Instead of positioning your approach in the spectrum defined by the two extremes you mention, you explain that it is built on top of hypertext - which is true, but to me seems to position it on another axis than the one previously defined. (Perhaps indeed there is a space of solutions, not a single axis.)

Definition 3: a facet is defined without any reference to a query or to a set of target articles. However, under Definition 3 you state that "the categories in the facet can reach the target articles": which target articles?

Section 4.1, the paragraph "The value of penalty... system evaluation" suffers from some small English errors (articles needed) but more seriously, is very unclear, as is Figure 4. How did you obtain the points which are used in the Figure? Manually picked articles/queries? other? what is the relevance of the 7 number obtained?

Section 4.2 (but also before): I do not understand "simultaneous navigation". Logically a user goes down or up in a hierarchy by refining/enlarging the focus. How can one "simultaneously navigate"?

Section 5.1, the RCH definition could be sharpened and formalized with the minimum number of words and with proper mathematical concepts (any tree whose nodes are ancestors or descendants of ... etc.)
Unclear: "which much large number of category-subcategory relationships."

Section 5.2 seems to have its end missing.

Section 5.3: "is tried to be replaced" could be rephrased

Section 6: erratic typing, undecided whether to leave a white space after a parenthesis (you should) or not (Section 6.2 doesn't).

Section 6.4.2: for lack of comments, the result is hard to interpret. Is this hierarchy OK? including the Justice Society for America?

Question 
Summary of the paper's main contributions and impact The paper presents an approach to generate facets for navigating in Wikipedia articles. The authors observe a human strategy to browse along facets starting with a simple keyword query in Wikipedia. Based in their findings they create a cost model to select and rank facets and to group facets into sub-graphs. They prove the efficiency and effectiveness of their prototype system in a human study and in a few performance experiments. As a result they claim to select facets with a higher precision than a competing approach called “CASTANET”.
Three strong points of the paper (please number them S1,S2,S3) 1. The paper includes a formal definition of the problem, of the cost metrics, ranking and search algorithms.
2. Findings are proven by experiments on a real data set from Wikipedia.
3. The authors name a broad selection of related work activities.
Three weak points of the paper (please number them W1,W2,W3) 1. The experiments (especially for selecting multi-facets) lack a statistically significant query set. Results of experiments are shown but not explained (E.g., Figure 5,8, 14-16).
2. The statement/content of Section 5.2. is unclear and incomplete.
3. The work does not explain, why the combined hill-climbing- and greedy-strategy solves the common problem of ranking multi-facets e.g., see Section 4.2.

Detailed comments (please number each point) • Figure 1 is not readable.

• Your area of related work is broad but not deep. Please explain why ranking facets over structured data is so much different from ranking facets over extracted data from Wikipedia? Do you incorporate uncertainty information in your algorithm our complex NLP operations?

• Section 4.2 what is your notion/intuition for using the Jaccard distance to measure the similarity of two paths within taxonomy? Why did you not investigate other probably better suited similarity metrics e.g., [3]

• Section 5.2. is incomplete.

• Section 5.3: How good does you algorithm scale to more than 200 nodes? What is the complexity?

• Section 5.3: Minor error: …is chosen by greedily by looking into …

• Section 6: Please use common metrics to measure your results e.g., the MRR or Success@X. Five queries are not representative. Please use more queries in your study.
