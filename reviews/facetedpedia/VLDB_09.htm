Summary of the paper's main contributions and impact (up to one paragraph) The subject of the article is to propose and evaluate a faceted user interface to browse wikipedia articles acquired using a keyword query. The main contributions are to propose automated technics 1 - to build facets and 2 - to select k of them to be integrated in a faceted interface.
Three strong points of the paper (please number them S1,S2,S3) S1 - an original idea
S2 - An automated process
Three weak points of the paper (please number them W1,W2,W3) W1 - a lack of analysis of desired properties (what is a good solution to your problem?)
W2 - a evaluation which is unclear for me
Detailed Comments (please number each point) First of all, I have to say that I think you develop an interesting idea. I would enjoy to use a faceted interface as you propose. But, after reading your article, some questions remain.

A - I start with a question about your approach. You use hyperlinks outgoing from a document as its attributes. Why not. But you don’t have any consideration about incoming hyperlinks. Why?

B - Another question is related to the user’s point of view. I agree with your claim: "missing the unreachable articles presents an unsatisfactory user experience". What you propose to avoid such an experience is unclear. Your evaluation of coverage of a facet shows that this problem is not addressed considering one facet. Ok, I think it will not be possible to address this problem using only one facet. You consider k-facets interfaces. The coverage may become total. If not, it means that some documents are unreachable which is quite bad. If yes, it seams to be a good property. What about your proposal? The coverage evaluation is conducted considering facets, but you don’t evaluate, nor discuss, the coverage of an interface! More generally, you don't clearly express which properties you expect.

C - I am also in trouble with your validation procedure. I consider you propose two kinds of validations: providing objective results (ex. coverage, average depth...) and a human evaluation (experimental results with human using your interface).
C-1 - The objective evaluation seems quite poor for two main reasons: first, very few objective evaluations are conducted, for example there is no performance consideration at all, and second, they only concern your proposal. You don’t even give evaluation for Castanet which is your challenger. As a consequence, real comparison and evaluation is quite difficult.
C-2 - The human evaluation leads to many questions:
C-2a - Do your proposal and your challenger Castanet use the same human interface?
C-2b - About users evaluation: they have three choices: two positive (relevant, very relevant), one negative (not relevant) but no neutral. Quite a surprising choice! Does this may have some impact on an human evaluation?
C-2c - how are participants selected? Who are they? According to your protocol, they have to be familiar with, or at least ask requests on, Nobel laureates, movies, country singers, european physicists, ... economic depression, database research,... chinese cuisine... superhero and global warming... quite impressive!
C-2d - Why do some experiments involve 60 users (figure 15) some others around 80 (figure 14) and some others around 400 (fig 9)?
C-2e - How many queries does a participant check for each experiment?
C-2f - Much more disturbing: participants have to choose queries among 20 possibilities. To conduct your experiments, who chooses the queries allocation to participants? How? How can a participant detect targeted articles for a query he does not choose himself? How much should we trust an evaluation by people who do not choose themselves the queries which results they judge?

D - I don’t understand why you limit users to a set of 20 possible queries. I see a large number of drawbacks about that, the only ‘advantage’ being for you to pre-compute many things which leads to think that performances are very poor. As far as you don’t present any complexity study nor performance measure...

To conclude, your present work should be great for a demo.

E1 - why do you change color conventions between figures 7-8 and 10-11? Quite confusing!

F2 - you disseminate some information which makes the article quite difficult to read.
Ex: In section 4.2 devoted to multi-facet ranking you require to jump to section 5.3 for integration of two criteria. In section 5.3 you make a proposal but conclude presenting other solutions. ‘Which one is the best?’, ‘Why?’ are then still open questions. Then you ask again the reader to jump to section 6 for an empirical comparison which is missing there.
Another example. «penality» parameter is introduced section 4.1, but its value is set in section 7 without any precise explanation.

Summary of the paper's main contributions and impact (up to one paragraph) This paper presents a faceted retrieval system for information discovery and exploration over Wikipedia. The main contributions of the paper are a metric for measuring the goodness of the facets, and algorithms for discovering the faceted interfaces.
Three strong points of the paper (please number them S1,S2,S3) S1 - The authors tackle the problem of faceted search by exploiting metadata available in Wikipedia (hyperlinks, categories) in a principled way.
S2 - Precise problem definition
S3 - Well defined ranking metrics
Three weak points of the paper (please number them W1,W2,W3) W1 - The "attributes" of the articles are obtained by following hyperlinks. It is not clear whether this is a reasonable strategy since there may be many hyperlinks in an article that are not strictly related to its meaning. For example, every article in Wikipedia contains a "Main Page" link. The authors do not discuss how to discard such irrelevant links.

W2 - Poorly designed experimental evaluation on a small number of arbitrarily selected queries.

W3 - The ranking metrics are based purely on the structure of the dag that comprises the taxonomy and the retrieved articles. However, the actual names of the categories should also be taken into account.
Detailed Comments (please number each point) 1 - The Mechanical Turk experiments do not show the effectiveness of the algorithms in terms of the proposed metrics. In particular, only some paths are shown to the Mechanical Turk judges. It might be the case that some of the paths that are not shown to them actually have higher relevance.

2 - In general, Mechanical Turk is useful for tasks that are very simple. It is not clear whether that is the case for the HITs used in this work. The design of the HITs is not explained. For example, what was the length of the paths shown to the judges? Were they shown the Wikipedia article directly or did they have to click on a link? Also, what was the inter-judge agreement?

Summary of the paper's main contributions and impact (up to one paragraph) This paper describes algorithms that compute the "best" multi-facet interface for wikipedia for a given search query. The paper relies on the category hierarchy in wikipedia in a fundmental way; (subsets) of the category hierarchy are the facets that are being combined. The paper also assumes that by exploiting the way in which articles are linked to other articles in wikipedia, one can assume that the categories assigned to the related articles (treated as attributes of the target papers) provide an indication of the categories of the target articles. The authors invent algorithms for finding the best k-faceted algorithms based on metrics that they devised to evaluate individual facets and multi-faceted interfaces. The authors performed user studies where users were asked to indicate whether or not a target article was, in fact, (not or highly or just) relevant to the original search query. The system described in this paper was compared (only) to a static system for generating multi-faceted systems called Castanet.
Three strong points of the paper (please number them S1,S2,S3) s1. The authors have completed a reasonable body of work; the algorithms and metrics are included in the paper.

s2. The authors conducted a user study to see if end-users found that articles were relevant to a (pre-supplied) search query.

s3. The paper contributes metrics to evaluate k-faceted interfaces.
Three weak points of the paper (please number them W1,W2,W3) w1. The biggest weakness of this paper is that it is limited to wikipedia. The work relies on/exploits characteristics that are true of wikipedia but may not be true of (any?) other digital library. These characteristics include: (1) The encyclopedic nature of the material; users submit articles precisely to be "about" particular topics. (2) The links among articles. Wikipedia articles are curated by wikipedia editors as well as the general public; any time a topic is mentioned in a wikipedia article it tends to be linked to the (existing) wikipedia article about that topic. (And remember that the articles tend to be "about" the topic.) (3) Wikipedia has a concept hierarchy. Although hierarchically structured controlled vocabularies (and in-depth material available to train people how to properly index documents using controlled vocabularies) have been around for decades and decades, generally, indexing according to a controlled vocabulary is not a particularly successful way to help users find documents of interest. The fact that articles in wikipedia are "indexed" according to multiple category hierarchies and that the indexing is of reasonably quality, is very unusual. As a result, I find it a bit difficult to see the technical material in this paper as addressing a general problem.

w2. Although the authors conduct a user study, I'm not necessarily convinced that a multi-faceted interface (built automatically from a single user query) is necessarily an effective way to help users find the best documents. Perhaps the success of their method has more to do with the nature of wikipedia than it does with information retrieval in general. How do we know that users will want to navigate all the way down the category/related document path? (The authors assume that they always will.) How do we know that relevant documents in the top 200 hits from the initial search that are NOT part of any of the existing category hierarchies should be ignored? As far as I understand, the multi-faceted interface excludes such documents. Perhaps in wikipedia, articles tend to be indexed by the category hierarchy ... but this certainly may not be true in general. How do we know that browsing through a multi-faceted interface finds the best document? The authors chose not to measure precision - at all - for example.

w3. The most important part of the paper - the specifics of how the multi-faceted interface is defined - is almost impossible to understand. The multi-faceted interface is described only in English. For example, the authors say "At each step, the available choices from both factes are put together as the choices in the integrated facets." It is impossible to tell whether they mean intersection or union. The examples seem to suggest that they mean intersection but imho the examples don't fully hang together. And what are the "choices"? Is is the set of target documents? Or the set of "attribute" documents? This is a serious deficiency of the paper in terms of readability.
Detailed Comments (please number each point) 1. The main metric about the "quality" of a path seems to be about the length of the path. This is indicated in the text of the paper in several places such as "Intuitively a low-cost navigational path, i.e. a page that demands small user effort, should have a small number of steps and require thats the user to browse a small number of choices." At least for this reader, it would have been more informative if you had explained the fanout a bit more; I was assuming path length as the main metric as I read. (I see, looking back, that you describe the fanout but it wasn't so obvious to me.)

2. It's not obvious to me that the path metric is useful. Your user study only had the user judge relevance of the target documents (the end point of the path) with the initial query. That provides no insight as to whether or not users would prefer bushy category hierarchies or long paths. Depending on the search/information need, one could imagine a busy/deep category hierarchy as being very informative - for articulating distinctions among various topics.

3. There are other, more direct ways to compute the intersection of documents that are reachable from multiple facets. One could imagine an alternative user interface for wikipedia or other collection where the user navigates several different category hierarchies and then (simply) asks for the intersection. That may be much more (or at least as) effective at helping the user find the right documents. One may not need to build a multi-faceted interface at all.

4. The paper brings up challenges that it assumes away. This seems like a writing style that is not very effective. Challenge 1 is "defining facets on wikipedia is non-trivial". But in fact, you (simply) use the category hierarchies of wikipedia directly. You mention that your work is different from work that is based on an IS-A or subsumption relationship because wikipedia uses a folksonomy. Yes, that's true; wikipedia is based on a folksonomy. But you end up relying on the hierarchically structured folksonomy exactly as if it were a hierarchical subsumption or isa relationship, as far as I can tell.

5. Some small comments: one section reference says "Section ??".

It seems unreasonable to assume that all articles are equally important.

It seems unreasonable to assume that small hierarchies are better; what about the suitability of the category to the query?

It is not clear to me why "after c8 is chosen, c12 and c13 are still valid choices but c11 is not available anymore because c11 cannot reach the target articles that c8 reaches. I may have made a mistake but I see that:
c2 reaches 1,2,3,4,5,6. c5 reaches 1,2,4,5,6,7. (So if it's intersection, then at this point we can reach 1, 2,4, 5, 6.) c8 reaches 3, 4, 5, 6. (If it's intersection, then at this point we can only reach 4, 5, 6.) c11 can only reach 1 so it's out. (fine.) But c12 reaches 4, 6, 7 and 7 is not in c8 (plus c12 doesn't have 3). and c13 only has 5. You can see that your basic explanation of the most important idea in the paper - the definition of the multi-faceted interface is not at all clear - at least to this reviewer.
